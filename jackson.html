The Age of Jackson

Democracy, Dissent, and Dispossession

The Rise of the Common Man

The election of Andrew Jackson marked a dramatic shift in American politics.

The "Corrupt Bargain" of 1824

The story of Jackson's presidency begins with his defeat in 1824. In that four-way election between Jackson, John Quincy Adams, Henry Clay, and William Crawford, Jackson won the most popular and electoral votes, but failed to secure the majority needed to win outright. The election was thrown to the House of Representatives. Henry Clay, as Speaker of the House and a political rival of Jackson, used his considerable influence to swing the vote in favor of Adams. Shortly after his victory, Adams appointed Clay as his Secretary of State, a position then seen as a stepping-stone to the presidency. Jackson's supporters were enraged, claiming a "corrupt bargain" had cheated their candidate and the will of the people. This accusation galvanized the Jacksonian movement and became their rallying cry for the next four years.

The Revolution of 1828 & Jacksonian Democracy

The 1828 election was a rematch and one of the most vicious in history. Jackson's supporters portrayed him as a rugged war hero and a man of the people, while Adams was painted as an out-of-touch elitist. Jackson won in a landslide. His victory represented the rise of the "common man" and a new political era. Jacksonian Democracy was characterized by a belief in the wisdom of the common person, the expansion of voting rights to virtually all white males (as property requirements were dropped in many states), and a deep suspicion of the wealthy and powerful, especially eastern bankers. Jackson championed the spoils system (also called rotation in office), rewarding his political supporters with government jobs. He argued it prevented the creation of a permanent, corrupt bureaucracy and made government more responsive to the people.

Indian Removal and the Trail of Tears

Jackson's presidency is forever stained by the forced relocation of Native American tribes.

The Indian Removal Act of 1830

For decades, the U.S. government had recognized tribes like the Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, Creek, and Seminole—known as the "Five Civilized Tribes"—as distinct nations. Many, especially the Cherokee, had adopted American customs, creating farms, schools, and a written constitution. However, southern states, hungry for their land for cotton cultivation, demanded their removal. Jackson, a veteran of campaigns against Native Americans, shared this view. In 1830, he pushed the Indian Removal Act through Congress. The act did not order removal by force, but authorized the president to grant land west of the Mississippi River to tribes that agreed to give up their ancestral homelands.1

Worcester v. Georgia and Jackson's Defiance

The Cherokee Nation refused to leave and challenged Georgia's attempts to seize their land in court. In the 1832 Supreme Court case Worcester v. Georgia, Chief Justice John Marshall sided with the Cherokee, ruling that the state of Georgia had no authority over them as they were a distinct political community. Upon hearing of the ruling, President Jackson allegedly remarked, "John Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it."2 While he may not have said those exact words, Jackson's actions spoke louder: he refused to enforce the Court's decision and continued to pressure the tribes to leave.

The Trail of Tears

Under immense pressure, a small, unauthorized faction of the Cherokee signed the Treaty of New Echota, selling all tribal land to the U.S. The vast majority of the Cherokee, led by Chief John Ross, protested, but the U.S. government ratified the fraudulent treaty. In 1838, the U.S. army began to forcibly round up the remaining 16,000 Cherokee. They were held in squalid camps before being forced to march over 1,000 miles to "Indian Territory" (modern-day Oklahoma). On this brutal journey, known as the Trail of Tears, an estimated 4,000 Cherokee—nearly a quarter of their population—died from disease, starvation, and exposure.

The Nullification Crisis

A battle over tariffs brought the nation to the brink of civil war.

The "Tariff of Abominations"

In 1828, Congress passed a very high protective tariff, which northern manufacturers favored but southern planters despised. Southerners, who relied on imported British goods, called it the "Tariff of Abominations" because it raised their costs and they felt it unfairly benefited the North at their expense. This economic grievance tapped into deeper southern fears about the power of the federal government to one day interfere with the institution of slavery.

Calhoun and the Doctrine of Nullification

In response, Vice President John C. Calhoun of South Carolina secretly wrote the "South Carolina Exposition and Protest." He resurrected the doctrine of nullification, first argued by Jefferson and Madison. Calhoun claimed that the states, having created the federal government, had the final say on the constitutionality of its laws. He argued that if a state found a federal law unconstitutional, it could declare that law "null and void" within its borders. This was a direct challenge to federal authority.

Showdown and Compromise

When Congress passed another tariff in 1832, South Carolina held a convention and formally nullified the federal law. They threatened to secede (withdraw from the Union) if the government tried to collect the taxes by force. President Jackson, though a supporter of states' rights, was a staunch nationalist who saw nullification as treason. He declared, "Disunion by armed force is treason. Are you ready to incur its guilt?" He asked Congress to pass a Force Bill authorizing him to use the military to enforce the law. A civil war seemed imminent, but Henry Clay, the "Great Compromiser," stepped in again. He engineered a compromise tariff that would gradually lower the rates over a decade. South Carolina accepted the compromise and repealed its nullification ordinance, ending the immediate crisis but leaving the fundamental question of states' rights versus federal power unresolved.3
