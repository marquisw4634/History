The Age of Adams

Crisis, Controversy, and the Constitution

Conflict with France

President Adams inherited a foreign policy crisis. France, embroiled in its own revolution, felt betrayed by American neutrality and its signing of the Jay Treaty with Great Britain.

The Quasi-War+
The French navy began seizing American merchant ships in the Atlantic. This led to an undeclared naval war from 1798 to 1800 known as the Quasi-War. Though never a formal war, it was a period of intense hostility and naval skirmishes.
The XYZ Affair+
To avoid full-scale war, Adams sent diplomats to Paris. They were met by secret agents of French Foreign Minister Talleyrand, referred to in reports as "X, Y, and Z." The agents demanded a bribe of $250,000 to even begin talks. The American envoy, Charles Cotesworth Pinckney, famously replied, "No, no, not a sixpence!" The news outraged Americans and fueled the slogan, "Millions for defense, but not one cent for tribute!"

Crisis at Home: The Alien & Sedition Acts

The Federalist-controlled Congress used the anti-French sentiment as a justification to pass four laws in 1798 designed to suppress domestic political opposition.

Federalists vs. Democratic-Republicans+
The two parties had fundamentally different visions. Federalists, like Adams and Hamilton, favored a strong central government, a manufacturing-based economy, and an alliance with Britain. Democratic-Republicans, like Jefferson and Madison, championed states' rights, an agrarian economy, and an alliance with France. The Federalists feared that immigrants, particularly from France and Ireland, would support their rivals.
The Alien & Sedition Acts Explained+
  • Naturalization Act: Increased residency for citizenship from 5 to 14 years.
  • Alien Friends Act: Allowed the president to deport any non-citizen deemed "dangerous."
  • Sedition Act: The most infamous, this act made it a crime to "write, print, utter or publish... any false, scandalous and malicious writing" against the government. Abigail Adams herself supported this, writing, "nothing will have an effect until Congress passes a Sedition Bill."
Targets of the Sedition Act+
The act was not used against spies, but against political opponents. At least 25 people, mostly Democratic-Republican newspaper editors, were arrested. The most famous case was Matthew Lyon, a Vermont Congressman who was re-elected to Congress from his jail cell after being imprisoned for criticizing President Adams.

The Virginia & Kentucky Resolutions

The Democratic-Republicans, led by Jefferson and Madison, saw the Acts as a flagrant violation of the Constitution and mounted a powerful counter-attack.

A Secret Rebellion+
Vice President Thomas Jefferson and his close ally James Madison knew they couldn't fight the acts in the Federalist-controlled federal courts. They turned to the state legislatures. Working in secret, Jefferson wrote a series of resolutions for Kentucky, and Madison wrote a similar set for Virginia.
The Doctrine of Nullification+
The resolutions introduced the radical idea of nullification, based on the "compact theory" that the states had created the federal government. Jefferson argued in the Kentucky Resolutions that when the federal government assumes un-delegated powers, "a nullification of the act is the rightful remedy." This meant states could declare federal laws unconstitutional and void within their borders.

Legacy and Consequences

While no other states formally adopted the Resolutions, their impact was profound and echoed through American history for decades to come.

The "Revolution of 1800"+
The public backlash against the Alien and Sedition Acts was a major factor in the presidential election of 1800. The Democratic-Republicans campaigned heavily on a platform of repealing the acts and protecting civil liberties. Jefferson's victory over Adams marked the first peaceful transfer of power between rival political parties, an event he called the "Revolution of 1800."
A Dangerous Precedent+
The idea of nullification did not go away. In the 1830s, South Carolina would attempt to nullify federal tariffs, leading to the Nullification Crisis. Ultimately, the arguments of states' rights and the right to reject federal law became a cornerstone of the secessionist argument that led to the American Civil War.
