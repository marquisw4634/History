üìúWhat Were the Articles, Really? A Wartime Plan!

Imagine it's the middle of the American Revolutionary War. The Second Continental Congress started drafting the Articles of Confederation in 1777, and they were finally ratified (approved) by all 13 states in 1781 ‚Äì while the war was still being fought! This was the first constitution (written plan of government) for the "United States of America."

Because they were created during a war against a powerful king, the Articles aimed to create a confederation ‚Äì a "league of friendship" where states worked together, especially for war, but kept most of their own power. The goal was to have enough unity to fight Britain, but not so much that the central government became a new tyrant.

üéØWhy Create Them? Winning the War & Staying Free!

The main job of the government under the Articles was to manage the Revolutionary War effort and deal with foreign countries. The colonists were fighting to escape the strong control of King George III, so they were terrified of creating another powerful national government.

  • Primary Goal: Successfully wage war against Great Britain and secure independence. This meant Congress needed powers to declare war, make treaties (like the alliance with France), raise an army (by asking states for troops), and borrow money for the war.
  • BIG Fear: A central government that could become oppressive, like the British monarchy. They wanted to protect the sovereignty (supreme power) of individual states.
  • The Compromise: A weak central government with specific, limited powers mostly related to war and diplomacy. States kept most of the day-to-day governing power.

üèõÔ∏èHow Was It Set Up? A Government for Limited Action

The government under the Articles was designed to be simple and have limited reach, reflecting wartime needs and fears:

The National Government (Congress):

  • One Branch Only: A unicameral (one-house) legislature called the Confederation Congress. No separate president (executive branch) or national court system (judicial branch).
  • One Vote Per State: Regardless of size or population, each state had one vote. This protected smaller states but could be unfair to larger ones.
  • Key Wartime Powers: Congress could:
    • Declare war and make peace.
    • Command the army and navy (but relied on states for soldiers).
    • Make treaties and alliances (crucial for foreign support like from France).
    • Borrow money and issue paper money (to fund the war).
    • Settle disputes between states (though this was difficult).

The State Governments:

  • Retained Most Power: States kept all powers not expressly delegated to Congress. This is a core idea of states' rights.
  • Controlled Taxation: Only states could tax their citizens directly to raise revenue. Congress had to request funds from states.
  • Raised Militias: States were responsible for raising and equipping their own militias (citizen armies) for local defense and to contribute to the national effort.

üò¨Post-War Problems & The Big Wake-Up Call!

While designed for wartime unity, the Articles had major weaknesses that became glaringly obvious after the Revolutionary War ended in 1783. The country faced new challenges, and the government wasn't equipped to handle them.

üí∏ No Power to Tax Directly

Congress could only request money from states. Many states paid little or nothing, leaving the national government broke and unable to pay war debts.

üî•The Crisis: Shays' Rebellion (1786-1787)

This uprising of farmers in Massachusetts became a symbol of the Articles' failures. Here's why it was such a big deal:

  • The Farmers' Plight: After the war, many farmers, including former soldiers, faced crushing debt. The Massachusetts government raised taxes to pay off its war debts, and demanded payment in hard currency (gold/silver), which was scarce. Farmers who couldn't pay lost their farms and sometimes faced debtors' prison.
  • The Rebellion: Led by Daniel Shays, a Revolutionary War veteran, these angry farmers began to protest. They shut down courts to prevent judges from foreclosing on farms. They wanted the state to print more paper money and pass laws to help debtors.
  • Weak National Response: Massachusetts asked the Confederation Congress for help to put down the rebellion. But Congress had no power to raise a national army without consent from other states, and no money to pay for one. It was largely powerless to act.
  • State Action & Aftermath: Eventually, the Massachusetts state militia, funded by wealthy Boston merchants, suppressed the rebellion. While Shays' followers were defeated, the event sent shockwaves across the country.
  • The Impact ‚Äì A Call for Change: Shays' Rebellion terrified many wealthy landowners, merchants, and national leaders like George Washington and James Madison. It exposed the dangerous weaknesses of the national government under the Articles. If the government couldn't maintain order and protect property, they feared the new nation could fall into anarchy (chaos, lack of government) or split apart. This fear was a major catalyst that led to the call for a convention in Philadelphia in 1787, originally to revise the Articles, but ultimately to replace them with the U.S. Constitution.

Shays' Rebellion dramatically highlighted that a stronger national government was needed to ensure domestic peace and stability.
